ETFs guide

Exchange-traded funds package an index or asset class into units you can buy like stocks. Learn types, costs, how they differ from index mutual funds, and review an educational SIP illustration.

What is an ETF?

An exchange-traded fund (ETF) holds a basket of securities (or commodities) and trades on the exchange during market hours at a live price. Most equity ETFs aim to track an index (e.g. Nifty 50) at low cost — combining mutual-fund-style diversification with stock-like trading.

Strengths: Diversification in one ticker, typically low expense ratios, transparent holdings, real-time quotes.
Caveats: Need Demat; bid–ask spreads and tracking difference matter; market risk of the underlying remains.

How to invest in ETFs

  1. Complete KYC and open a Demat + trading account with a SEBI-registered broker.
  2. Search the ticker (e.g. broad Nifty ETFs often discussed as NIFTYBEES-style products).
  3. Prefer limit orders near fair value; check AUM, expense ratio, and tracking difference.
  4. Hold in Demat; sell during market hours like a stock.
  5. For automatic SIPs, many people use index mutual funds instead — compare below.

Costs & tracking (checklist)

  • Expense ratio — ongoing fee in NAV
  • Tracking difference — how much the ETF lags (or leads) its index over time
  • Bid–ask spread — trading cost on the exchange
  • Liquidity / AUM — thin ETFs can be costly to enter/exit
  • Tax — equity vs non-equity classification matters; rules change — Tax overview

ETF vs Index Mutual Fund: A Quick Comparison

Feature ETF Index Mutual Fund
How to Buy On stock exchange (requires Demat account) Directly from AMC/platform (no Demat needed)
Pricing Real-time, like shares Once daily (end-of-day NAV)
Expense Ratio Very low (0.05–0.2%) Low (0.1–0.5%)
Liquidity High (depends on trading volume) Always redeemable at NAV
SIP Option No (but you can manually invest monthly) Yes, automated SIPs

ETF SIP Returns Calculator – FAQs

What is SIP in ETFs?
SIP (Systematic Investment Plan) is a disciplined way of investing a fixed amount regularly (e.g., monthly) into an ETF, just like in mutual funds. In practice, you buy ETF units each month via your broker, accumulating units over time.
What is XIRR and why is it important for SIP?
XIRR (Extended Internal Rate of Return) gives you the annualized return for a series of cash flows occurring at different dates. For SIPs, it accurately reflects your real return by considering each investment’s timing, which plain CAGR does not.
Why is ETF SIP XIRR different from index return?
Index returns are based on a lump-sum investment at the start. SIP XIRR considers that you buy at different prices (sometimes high, sometimes low) and thus get a “rupee cost averaging” benefit. The SIP XIRR can be lower or higher than index CAGR, depending on market trends.
Can I do SIP in ETFs automatically?
Most brokers in India do not yet offer automated SIP for ETFs (unlike mutual funds). You need to manually buy ETF units each month, but some discount brokers allow scheduled “buy reminders” or auto-invest via APIs.
Why does the final corpus differ between ETFs?
The difference comes from each ETF’s price growth and volatility. Equity ETFs like NIFTYBEES may outperform gold ETFs like GOLDBEES over long periods, but short-term returns may vary.
Is there any tax on ETF SIP returns?
Tax depends on whether the ETF is treated as equity-oriented or not under current law, your holding period, and the Finance Act for that year. Rules change — see our Tax overview and official Income Tax sources. Not tax advice.
Is XIRR the best metric to track my real SIP performance?
Yes! XIRR is the industry-standard method to measure actual investor returns for SIP or any staggered investment, as it adjusts for both timing and amount of cash flows.

Who often uses ETFs?

  • Investors with Demat accounts seeking low-cost index exposure
  • People comfortable placing exchange orders (or limit orders)
  • Those building a core passive sleeve alongside SIPs in mutual funds
Tip: Want auto-debit SIPs with no Demat? Index mutual funds may feel simpler. Want exchange liquidity and very low costs? ETFs may fit — compare total costs, not just TER.

Common mistakes

  • Ignoring tracking difference and spreads while chasing the lowest TER alone
  • Using thin sector ETFs as a “diversified core”
  • Trading ETFs like day-trading stocks without a plan
  • Assuming tax rules never change — re-check equity vs non-equity classification

FAQs

Are ETFs “safe”?
SEBI-regulated products still carry market risk of the underlying. Safety ≠ guaranteed returns.
Can I SIP an ETF?
Many brokers lack automatic SIP; investors often buy monthly manually or use index mutual funds for automation.
Minimum investment?
Usually one unit — price depends on the ETF’s market quote.
