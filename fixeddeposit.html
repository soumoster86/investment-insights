Fixed deposits guide & calculator

Learn how bank FDs work, what DICGC covers, estimate maturity with different compounding choices, and sketch a simple FD ladder. This is for learning only, not a rate offer from any bank.

What is a fixed deposit?

A fixed deposit (FD) is money parked with a bank or NBFC for a chosen tenure at a stated interest rate. For that rate and compounding, you usually know the maturity amount up front. That makes FDs popular for short-to-medium goals and safer parts of a portfolio. Interest is usually taxable, and after inflation it may lag long-run equity returns.

Strengths: Predictable rate for the tenure, simple product, optional early break (often with a penalty), and senior-citizen or tax-saving variants at many banks.
Limits: After tax the rate may not beat inflation. Early exit can cost you. NBFC and bank credit risk differ. DICGC insurance has a per-bank cap (see below).

Common FD types

Same “fixed deposit” label, but different locks, rates, and payout styles. Product rules always matter more than the label.

💰

Regular / cumulative

Lump sum in; interest compounds and is paid at maturity (or as per the payout option you choose).

Often used for General savings goals

🧾

Tax-saving FD

Usually a 5-year lock-in; often discussed under Section 80C-style options.

Often used for Tax planning with a forced lock-in (not advice)

🧓

Senior citizen FD

Higher rate bands for eligible seniors (bank rules apply).

Often used for Retirement cash-flow needs

🔄

Flexi / sweep

Links your savings balance to automatic FDs.

Often used for Idle cash when you still want some access

📅

Recurring deposit (RD)

Monthly deposits instead of one lump sum.

Often used for Salary savers. Try the RD calculator

Safety & DICGC (high level)

Deposits with insured banks are covered by the Deposit Insurance and Credit Guarantee Corporation (DICGC) up to a notified limit per bank per depositor (commonly discussed as ₹5 lakh including principal and interest). Confirm the current limit on official RBI/DICGC sources. Coverage does not make every NBFC deposit the same as a bank FD.

  • 🏛️Prefer regulated banks and NBFCs you understand
  • 🛡️Don’t put more than the insured limit in one bank if insurance is your main safety plan
  • 📄Read premature-withdrawal and auto-renewal terms

Tax notes on FD interest (high level)

Not tax advice. Rules, TDS thresholds, and forms change. Confirm with the Income Tax Department or a qualified professional.
  • 💼Interest is generally taxable under the head that applies to you (often as income from other sources).
  • 📅Banks may deduct TDS when interest exceeds notified thresholds. Figures change; verify for the year.
  • 💼Form 15G/15H comes up when total income is below taxable limits. Eligibility is personal.
  • 📅Tax-saving FDs and Section 80C rules depend on the regime and law for that year. See the tax overview and PPF & tax-saving.

Common mistakes

  • 🔒Locking emergency money in long FDs with no plan for an early break
  • 🧾Ignoring tax and comparing pre-tax FD rates with equity returns
  • 🔁Auto-renewing forever without matching the money to a goal
  • ⚠️Chasing the highest NBFC rate without understanding risk and insurance

Practical tips

  • 🪜Ladder maturities so cash becomes available every year or quarter.
  • 📈Prefer cumulative FDs if you do not need periodic interest payouts.
  • 🛟Keep some money liquid for true emergencies. See the emergency fund guide.
  • ⚖️For long-term growth, pair FDs with diversified equity or funds. See Risk & allocation.

FAQs

Can I break an FD early?
Usually yes, often with a penalty and a reduced rate. Check your bank.
Is interest taxable?
Generally yes, as per your status and current law. Not tax advice.
Cumulative vs non-cumulative?
Cumulative compounds to maturity; non-cumulative pays interest periodically.
FD vs debt fund?
FD rate is contractual for the deposit; debt fund NAV can move with markets.
