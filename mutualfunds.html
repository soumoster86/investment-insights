Mutual funds guide & calculators

Understand fund types, SIPs and SWPs, run transparent projections, and shortlist sample schemes for research — educational only, not a recommendation to buy any fund.

What is a mutual fund?

A mutual fund pools money from many investors and invests in a portfolio of securities (equities, debt, gold, etc.) according to a stated mandate. You own units priced at a daily NAV (net asset value). Compared with picking many individual stocks yourself, funds offer built-in diversification and professional (or rules-based index) management — with costs and market risk still applying.

Why people use them: Diversification, SIPs for discipline, low ticket size, and easy access via apps / AMCs / MF Utility.
What can go wrong: NAV can fall, active managers can underperform, expense ratios compound against you, and past returns do not guarantee the future.

SIP vs lumpsum (concept)

Aspect SIP Lumpsum
Cash flow Fixed amount at regular intervals One-time investment
Behaviour Builds habit; averages purchase NAV over time Depends on entry level and staying invested
Who often uses Salaried / monthly savers Bonus, inheritance, or rebalancing cash
Neither is “always better.” Match the method to when money is available and your horizon. Try numbers with the SIP calculator below or Lumpsum / Goal planner.

How to invest (practical path)

  1. Complete KYC (PAN, Aadhaar, bank) on a regulated platform or AMC app.
  2. Choose a category that matches goal and risk (not last year’s top return alone).
  3. Compare expense ratio, AUM, tracking (for index), and consistency — not star ratings alone.
  4. Start a SIP or lumpsum you can stick with; keep proof of investments.
  5. Review once or twice a year; avoid chasing every new fund launch.

Key terms

  • NAV — price per unit (usually updated each business day).
  • Expense ratio — ongoing fee embedded in returns.
  • Exit load — fee if you redeem within a stated period.
  • Direct vs regular — commission channel affects cost.
  • AUM — assets under management (size of the fund).
  • Benchmark — index used to judge performance.
  • SWP — systematic withdrawal of a fixed amount.
  • STP — systematic transfer between schemes (product rules vary).

Common mistakes

  • Owning many overlapping equity funds that all hug the same index (high fees, low diversification benefit)
  • Chasing last year’s top performer and rotating every year (tax + behaviour drag)
  • Ignoring expense ratio and direct-plan costs
  • Stopping SIPs only because markets fell (if the horizon is still long)
  • Using equity funds for money needed in a few months

Practical tips

  • Start early and stay consistent — SIPs work best with time, not constant tinkering.
  • Match equity share to horizon — Risk & allocation.
  • Prefer a simple core (e.g. broad index or diversified active) before niche sector funds.
  • Review once or twice a year; rebalance if your plan weights drift.
  • Pair goals with numbers — Goal planner.
