PPF & tax-saving basics

A plain-language overview of Public Provident Fund (PPF) and common Section 80C-style options in India. Not tax advice — rules change; confirm with official sources or a qualified professional.

What is PPF?

Public Provident Fund (PPF) is a long-term savings scheme backed by the Government of India. Individuals can open an account at participating banks or post offices and deposit money each year (within notified limits).

Interest rates are set by the government and can change over time. PPF is often discussed for long-horizon, relatively stable compounding — not for short-term trading or high growth.

Features people usually care about

  • Long lock-in: Classic PPF has a multi-year tenure (commonly discussed as 15 years) with extension options under rules.
  • Deposit band: Minimum and maximum yearly deposits are prescribed (e.g. many guides cite ₹500 minimum and ₹1.5 lakh maximum — verify current limits).
  • Interest: Credited as per scheme rules; rate is not the same as equity market returns.
  • Partial withdrawals / loans: Allowed only under specific conditions and timelines.
  • Tax treatment: Historically associated with EEE-style treatment for eligible contributions/interest/maturity under prevailing law — always re-check current tax law.
Try numbers: Use our simplified PPF calculator to see how annual deposits might grow under an assumed rate. It is an educational model, not an official quote.

Who often looks at PPF?

  • Investors wanting a long-term, relatively predictable savings sleeve
  • People already using equity SIPs who want a debt-like core
  • Those exploring options commonly listed under tax-saving discussions

PPF may be less suitable if you need money soon, want high market-linked growth, or prefer full liquidity.

Section 80C landscape (very high level)

Indian tax law has historically allowed deductions up to a combined limit (often discussed as ₹1.5 lakh under Section 80C and related clauses) for eligible investments and expenses. Common names that appear in this conversation include:

  • PPF contributions
  • ELSS mutual funds (market-linked, lock-in typically 3 years)
  • Life insurance premiums (product-dependent)
  • EPF / certain deposits / tuition fees / principal repayment on home loans (context-specific)
  • NPS has additional clauses (e.g. discussions around 80CCD) beyond plain 80C
Important: Eligibility, limits, and tax treatment depend on your status, the financial year, and current law. This site does not prepare returns or certify deductions. Use official Income Tax materials or a qualified tax professional.

Practical cautions

  • Do not invest only because of a tax deduction — cash flow and goals come first.
  • Do not lock money you may need for emergencies into long lock-ins.
  • Compare costs and lock-ins before buying insurance-linked “investment” products.
  • Keep proof of deposits and account statements each year.
