How to start investing in India

A practical path from zero to your first SIP — accounts, KYC, and sensible first steps. Educational overview only; not personalised financial advice.

Why invest at all?

Money sitting only in a savings account often grows slower than prices rise (inflation). Investing means putting money into assets — such as mutual funds, stocks, deposits, or bonds — that can help your savings work toward goals like an emergency buffer, a home, education, or retirement.

Mindset: Start small, stay consistent, and prefer long horizons (years, not days) while you are learning.

Do this before you invest

  1. Build a basic emergency fund. Aim for a few months of essential expenses in a liquid place (savings account or liquid fund) so you are less likely to sell investments in a crisis.
  2. Clear high-interest consumer debt (for example credit-card dues) when interest costs outweigh typical investment returns.
  3. Protect dependants with term life insurance if needed — insurance is for protection, not a substitute for investing.
  4. Write down goals (amount + year). Use the Goal planner to sketch a SIP.

Accounts you typically need

Bank account

A regular savings account linked to UPI/net banking is the base for payments and SIP auto-debits.

KYC (Know Your Customer)

KYC verifies your identity (usually PAN, Aadhaar, photo, signature/eSign). Complete KYC once with a mutual fund platform, broker, or KRA so you can invest across products.

Demat & trading account (for stocks, ETFs, many bonds)

  • Demat holds securities electronically (like a locker for shares).
  • Trading is the account you use to place buy/sell orders on the exchange.
  • Opened together via a SEBI-registered broker (examples people use: Zerodha, Groww, Upstox, bank brokers, etc.).

Mutual fund folio (can be without Demat)

You can start many mutual fund SIPs with only KYC + bank account through a fund house app, MF utility, or investment platform — Demat is optional for mutual funds.

Safety: Prefer regulated platforms. Never share OTPs or passwords. Treat unsolicited “guaranteed return” tips as a red flag.

A sensible first investment: SIP in a diversified fund

For many beginners, a Systematic Investment Plan (SIP) in a diversified equity or hybrid mutual fund (or a broad market index fund/ETF once you have a Demat) is simpler than picking individual stocks.

  1. Finish KYC on a trusted platform.
  2. Choose a fund category you understand (e.g. large-cap / flexi-cap / index — research expense ratio and risk).
  3. Start a SIP amount you can continue even in a bad month.
  4. Automate the debit date; review once or twice a year, not every day.

Try numbers first with the SIP calculator and Target SIP tool.

Beginner checklist

  • PAN and bank account ready
  • KYC completed
  • Emergency money set aside
  • Goal written (even roughly)
  • First SIP set up (small is fine)
  • Know how to log in and find statements/folios

Common early mistakes

  • Investing money needed in the next few months into equities
  • Chasing last year’s top-performing fund or hot tips
  • Checking portfolio daily and panic-stopping SIPs
  • Buying many overlapping funds (“collection,” not a plan)
  • Mixing insurance and investment products without understanding costs
