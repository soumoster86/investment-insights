Emergency fund & insurance basics

Before chasing higher returns, most households benefit from a cash buffer and the right protection cover. This guide explains the ideas — not product recommendations or personalised advice.

Why “protect first” matters

Investing works best when you can stay invested through job loss, medical bills, or family emergencies. Without a buffer, people often redeem mutual funds or sell stocks at the worst time. Insurance (life and health) is meant to transfer large, rare risks — it is not a substitute for SIPs or a way to “beat the market.”

Order of operations (typical): clear toxic high-interest debt → build an emergency fund → buy essential protection if dependants or medical risk apply → then invest for goals. See also How to start.

Emergency fund

What it is

Money set aside for unexpected essential expenses — not holidays, gadgets, or market “dips.” It should be easy to access without brokerage delays or big price risk.

How much people often target

A common educational range is 3–12 months of essential expenses (rent/EMI, food, utilities, school fees, insurance premiums, minimum debt payments). Single-income households, freelancers, and people with dependents often aim higher; dual stable incomes with low fixed costs may start lower and grow into it.

Where to keep it

  • Savings account — most liquid; returns usually low.
  • Sweep / liquid-style options — slightly better yield with quick access (product rules vary).
  • Short FDs or liquid/overnight funds — used by some for the outer layers of the buffer; still prioritise access and low volatility.
Usually not ideal for the core buffer: equity funds, individual stocks, crypto, or long-lock products (e.g. classic PPF) where you cannot get money quickly without cost or penalty.

How to build it

  1. List monthly essentials (honestly, not aspirational lifestyle spend).
  2. Open a dedicated account or earmark a balance so you do not “accidentally” spend it.
  3. Automate a transfer on salary day until the target is reached.
  4. After you use it, refill before increasing risk investments.

Rough sizing: use the Goal planner or Target SIP with a short horizon and conservative assumptions — treat results as sketches only.

Term life insurance (concept)

Term cover pays a lump sum to nominees if the insured person dies during the policy term. Premiums are typically lower than for policies that mix insurance with investment or savings features, because you are mainly buying pure risk cover.

  • Who often needs it: people with dependents who rely on their income, or outstanding loans that family would struggle to service.
  • Who may need less (or none): people with no financial dependents and adequate assets — still a personal judgment, not a rule.
  • Cover amount (idea): often discussed as a multiple of annual expenses/income plus major liabilities, minus existing liquid assets — work this through carefully or with a licensed adviser.
Education tip: Compare claim settlement ratios, exclusions, and insurer stability from public sources. Prefer clear term plans over opaque “returns + cover” bundles unless you fully understand costs.

Health insurance (concept)

Medical costs can wipe out years of SIPs. A health policy is designed to pay (or reimburse) eligible hospitalisation and related expenses per policy terms — not to grow wealth.

  • Check sum insured, room-rent limits, co-pay, waiting periods, and network hospitals.
  • Employer cover is helpful but may end if you change jobs; many people keep a personal policy as a base.
  • Family floater vs individual policies trade off premium vs how the sum insured is shared.

Rules, IRDAI product norms, and tax treatment of premiums can change. Read the policy wording and official insurer materials; this site does not sell or rate specific policies.

Insurance vs investing

Aspect Protection (term / health) Investing (SIP, stocks, FD…)
Main job Transfer large financial risks Grow or park money for goals
Success looks like Claim paid when needed; sleep better Corpus toward goals after costs & tax
Common confusion Buying cover mainly for “returns” Using equities as the only emergency fund

Illustrative. Product features vary widely.

Many traditional savings–insurance hybrids exist. They can be appropriate for some people, but costs, lock-ins, and returns should be understood separately from pure term cover and pure investments. See also PPF & tax-saving basics.

Common mistakes

  • Skipping the emergency fund to maximise equity SIPs early on
  • Under-insuring health while over-buying market-linked “ULIP-style” products without comparing costs
  • Assuming employer cover is permanent
  • Naming no nominee, or never telling family where policies and accounts live
  • Treating insurance commission-driven sales pitches as independent advice
