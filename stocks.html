What is a stock?

A stock (share) is a unit of ownership in a company. When you buy shares listed on exchanges like the NSE or BSE, you can benefit if the business grows (price appreciation) and, for some companies, from dividends. Prices move every trading day — sometimes a lot — so equities suit money you can leave invested for years, not emergency cash.

Upside (why people invest): Long-term growth potential, liquidity on exchanges, and participation in Indian (and global) businesses.
Downside (risks): Market and company risk, no guaranteed returns, and possible permanent loss if a business fails.

Large, mid & small caps (idea)

SEBI and index providers classify companies by size. Labels change over time — treat this as a map, not a ranking.

Bucket Typical traits Often used for Risk tone
Large-cap Bigger, more established names (e.g. many Nifty 50 members) Core long-term equity Relatively lower vs smaller peers (still volatile)
Mid-cap Growing companies between large and small Growth sleeve Medium–high
Small-cap Smaller businesses, thinner liquidity at times Satellite / high-risk sleeve High

Prefer diversified funds/ETFs if picking individual small-caps feels overwhelming — see Index vs active and ETFs.

Orders & metrics beginners hear about

Order types (simplified)

  • Market order — buy/sell at the best available price now (speed over price control).
  • Limit order — you set a max buy or min sell price; may not fill.
  • Stop-loss (concept) — an order style meant to limit losses if price moves against you (exact product names vary by broker).

Ratios (glossary, not magic numbers)

  • Market cap — share price × shares outstanding (size proxy).
  • P/E — price relative to earnings; high/low alone is incomplete without growth and peers.
  • EPS — earnings per share; can be noisy year to year.
  • Dividend yield — dividend ÷ price; past yields can fall if price rises or dividends are cut.
Practice: Read a company’s annual report / exchange filings and compare with peers before acting. Screeners and social tips are starting points, not conclusions.

How to invest in stocks in India

  1. PAN + bank account ready for KYC.
  2. Open a Demat + trading account with a SEBI-registered broker.
  3. Complete KYC (eSign / video KYC as offered).
  4. Fund the account and place a small practice order you understand.
  5. Prefer a written plan: goal, horizon, max loss you can tolerate, and diversification rules.

Starter checklist

  • Emergency fund and high-interest debt handled first — see Emergency & insurance
  • Know the difference between investing and intraday speculation — Intraday overview
  • Consider broad index ETFs/funds before a long list of single stocks — ETFs
  • Understand tax basics at a high level — Tax overview

Common mistakes

  • Buying tips without understanding the business or your time horizon
  • Concentrating most savings in one stock or one sector
  • Using leverage / F&O without experience (high ruin risk)
  • Checking prices every hour and panic-selling long-term holdings
  • Ignoring costs, taxes, and the need for an emergency fund

Practical tips

  • Match equity exposure to years until you need the money — Risk & allocation.
  • Prefer a simple diversified core; use single stocks only for amounts you can research.
  • Review yearly, not daily; rebalance if weights drift far from your plan.
  • Write rules before market stress (what you’ll buy, hold, or sell).
