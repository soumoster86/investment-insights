IPO guide for Indian investors

How initial public offerings work, how retail applications usually flow, and why listing-day excitement is not the same as a long-term plan. For learning only. Not advice to apply for any issue.

What is an IPO?

An initial public offering (IPO) is when a private company offers shares to the public and lists on an exchange (NSE/BSE). Investors apply during a short window at a price band. Allotment depends on demand and category rules (retail, HNI, QIB, and so on).

Why people apply: A chance to own a newly listed business, and possible listing gains when demand is strong.
Why many skip or keep bids small: Overpricing risk, limited track record as a listed firm, lottery-style allotment, and sharp moves after listing.

How an IPO works (simplified)

  1. 📄Offer document: The company files a DRHP / RHP path with SEBI disclosures.
  2. 💰Price band and dates are announced. Apply via ASBA/UPI through brokers or banks.
  3. 📊Subscription and allotment are finalised per category rules.
  4. 📈Listing: Shares credit to Demat, and trading begins at a free market price.

Risks

  • ⚠️Valuation risk: The issue price may already assume optimistic growth.
  • ⚠️Listing volatility: First-day moves can be large either way.
  • ⚠️Allotment risk: Oversubscribed retail issues often allot only partly or not at all.
  • ⚠️Information risk: There is less public trading history than for seasoned stocks.
  • 📁Lock-in and promoter structure: Read the offer document carefully.

How to apply (typical retail flow)

  1. 🪪Get ready: PAN, Demat, and bank/UPI with a SEBI-registered broker.
  2. 📱Bid in the window: Choose lots and a price within the band on the app.
  3. ✅Approve the UPI/ASBA mandate on time.
  4. 📬Check allotment after close. The fund block is released if you are not allotted.
  5. 🧭If allotted: Sell on listing day or hold with a clear plan.

Pre-apply checklist

  • 📄Read risks and the business model in the offer document, not only social-media summaries
  • 🧐Compare valuation with peers and treat growth claims with healthy scepticism
  • 💰Size the bid as money you can lock for a short time and risk after listing
  • 🛟Do not fund IPO bids by draining emergency cash. See Emergency fund
  • 🧺A long-term core still belongs in diversified equity: ETFs / Mutual funds

FAQs about IPOs

What is the minimum amount needed to apply for an IPO?
Usually the minimum bid is one lot (often 10–15 shares). The cost depends on the price band. Many mainboard IPOs need about ₹15,000–₹20,000 for one lot.
How are IPO shares allotted?
If demand exceeds supply, allotment is by lottery. If the issue is under-subscribed, all valid bids may get full allotment.
Can I sell IPO shares on listing day?
Yes. Once shares are credited to your Demat, you can sell anytime during market hours.
Is IPO investment risky?
Yes. IPOs can be volatile, and past listing gains do not guarantee future results. Read the DRHP and the company’s basics before you apply.
Are IPO gains taxable?
Generally yes under capital gains rules for equities when you sell. Holding period and rates depend on current law. See Tax overview. Not tax advice.

Common mistakes

  • 📢Applying only because “GMP” or social media hype is high
  • 🏠Using money needed for rent, EMIs, or emergencies
  • 🎲Selling or holding on listing day with no rule decided in advance
  • 📄Ignoring RHP risks while staring only at subscription numbers
