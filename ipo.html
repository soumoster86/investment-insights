IPO guide for Indian investors

Learn how initial public offerings work, how retail applications typically flow, and why listing-day excitement is not the same as a long-term plan. Educational only — not advice to apply for any issue.

What is an IPO?

An initial public offering (IPO) is when a private company offers shares to the public and lists on an exchange (NSE/BSE). Investors apply during a short window at a price band; allotment depends on demand and category rules (retail, HNI, QIB, etc.).

Why people apply: Participation in a newly listed business; possible listing gains when demand is strong.
Why many skip or size small: Overpricing risk, limited track record as a listed firm, allotment lottery, and sharp post-listing swings.

How an IPO works (simplified)

  1. Company files a draft offer document (DRHP / RHP path) with SEBI disclosures.
  2. Price band and issue dates are announced; investors apply via ASBA/UPI through brokers/banks.
  3. Demand (subscription) is published; allotment is finalised per category rules.
  4. Shares credit to Demat; listing day trading begins at a free market price.

Risks

  • Valuation risk — issue price may already embed optimistic growth.
  • Listing volatility — first-day moves can be large either way.
  • Allotment risk — oversubscribed retail issues often allot partially or not at all.
  • Information risk — less public trading history than seasoned stocks.
  • Lock-in / promoter dynamics — read the offer document for structure.

How to apply (typical retail flow)

  1. Have PAN, Demat, bank/UPI ready with a SEBI-registered broker.
  2. During the window, open the IPO on the app and enter bid (lots × price within band).
  3. Approve the UPI/ASBA mandate in time.
  4. Check allotment status after the issue closes; funds block releases if not allotted.
  5. If allotted, decide whether listing-day trade fits your plan — or hold with a thesis.

Pre-apply checklist

  • Read risks and business model in the offer document (not only social-media summaries)
  • Compare valuation vs peers and growth claims with healthy scepticism
  • Size the application as money you can lock briefly and risk after listing
  • Don’t fund IPO bids by skipping emergency cash — Emergency fund
  • Long-term core still belongs in diversified equity — ETFs / Mutual funds

FAQs about IPOs

What is the minimum amount needed to apply for an IPO?
Usually, the minimum bid is one lot (often 10–15 shares). The cost depends on the price band (e.g., ₹15,000–₹20,000 for most mainboard IPOs).
How are IPO shares allotted?
Allotment is via lottery if demand exceeds supply. If under-subscribed, all valid bids may get full allotment.
Can I sell IPO shares on listing day?
Yes, once shares are credited to your Demat, you can sell anytime during market hours.
Is IPO investment risky?
Yes. IPOs can be volatile and past performance is no guarantee of future results. Review the DRHP and company fundamentals before investing.
Are IPO gains taxable?
Generally yes under capital gains rules for equities when sold — holding period and rates depend on current law. See Tax overview. Not tax advice.

Common mistakes

  • Applying only because “GMP” or social media hype is high
  • Using money needed for rent, EMIs, or emergencies
  • Selling or holding on listing day without a pre-written rule
  • Ignoring RHP risks while focusing only on subscription numbers
