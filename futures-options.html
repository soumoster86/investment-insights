Futures & options (F&O)

A plain guide to what futures and options are, how they differ from buying shares, and why most beginners should treat them with care. This is for learning only. It is not a trading course, and not personal advice.

What is F&O?

Futures and options are derivatives. Their price depends on something else, often called the underlying. In India that is commonly an index (Nifty, Bank Nifty) or a stock. You do not need to own the shares to trade the contract, but you still take real market risk, and you often use margin.

Why people use them: Hedge a portfolio, express a short-term view with less cash up front, or (for some) try to earn premium by selling options.
Why they hurt beginners: Leverage, time decay on options, complex greeks, and the chance of large losses (especially if you sell options without understanding the risk).

F&O on NSE is regulated and settled under exchange rules. Eligibility, margins, and product lists change. Always check your broker and exchange materials. If you are still building an emergency fund or a simple SIP habit, start with How to start and Stocks first.

Futures vs options (idea map)

Same family of products, different risk shapes. Simplified for teaching.

📈

Futures

What it is
A binding agreement to buy or sell the underlying at a set price on a future date
Obligation
Both sides are committed (unless they close the position earlier)
Upfront cost idea
Margin (not the full notional value), marked to market daily

Gains and losses can grow large either way

🎫

Options

What it is
A contract that gives the buyer a right, not a duty, to buy (call) or sell (put) at a strike
Obligation
Buyer can walk away; seller (writer) may have to fulfil if exercised
Upfront cost idea
Buyer pays a premium; sellers post margin and take on more open-ended risk

Buyers risk the premium; sellers can face larger losses

How futures work (simple picture)

  1. 📋Contract specs matter. Lot size, expiry, and the underlying are fixed by the exchange.
  2. 💰You post margin, not the full contract value. That is leverage. A small move in the index can mean a large gain or loss relative to the cash you put up.
  3. 📅Mark-to-market (MTM) settles daily. Profits and losses hit your account as the futures price moves.
  4. 🔄Most traders square off before expiry. Holding to settlement has its own rules and risks.
Margin calls: If the market moves against you, the broker can ask for more money or close the position. Plan for that before you trade, not after.

How options work (calls and puts)

📞

Call option

The buyer of a call hopes the underlying rises above the strike (enough to cover the premium). The most you can lose as a buyer is the premium you paid, if you let it expire worthless.

🛡️

Put option

The buyer of a put hopes the underlying falls below the strike (enough to cover the premium). Puts are also used as a form of downside protection when you already hold shares.

  • ⏱️Time value decays. Options lose value as expiry nears if nothing else changes. That hurts many option buyers who are “almost right” but too late.
  • 📊Implied volatility is priced into the premium. When fear rises, option prices often jump even if the index has not moved much yet.
  • ⚖️Buying vs selling: Buying limits loss to the premium. Selling can collect premium but can face large losses if the market runs hard against the short position.

Key terms

  • 🎯Underlying — the index or stock the contract is based on.
  • 📦Lot size — how many units one contract represents (set by the exchange).
  • 📌Strike — the price level in an option contract.
  • 💵Premium — what an option buyer pays (and a seller receives) for the contract.
  • 🏦Margin — cash or collateral the broker holds against your open risk.
  • 📆Expiry — when the contract ends; weekly and monthly cycles exist for many products.
  • 📐ITM / ATM / OTM — in, at, or out of the money relative to the strike and spot.
  • ⚡Leverage — controlling a large notional with a smaller cash outlay; losses scale the same way.

Risks to take seriously

  • 📉Leverage risk — small market moves can wipe out a large share of the cash you posted as margin.
  • ⏳Time decay — long options can lose value even when your direction call is eventually right.
  • 🔥Short option risk — selling naked calls or puts can produce losses far larger than the premium you took in.
  • 💧Liquidity and spreads — thin strikes are costly to enter and exit.
  • 🧾Costs and tax — brokerage, STT-style charges, GST, and tax treatment differ from delivery equity. See Tax overview.
  • 🧠Behaviour — revenge trading after a loss is common and expensive.

Who often looks at F&O (and who should wait)

  • 🛡️Hedgers with a clear portfolio to protect (for example buying puts against a large equity holding).
  • 📚Traders who already understand cash equities, position sizing, and can afford to lose the capital they risk.
  • 🚫Usually a poor first step if you still need an emergency fund, have high-interest debt, or have never held a simple index fund SIP. Prefer Emergency & insurance and Mutual funds first.
Educational rule of thumb: Money for F&O should be money you can lose without touching rent, EMIs, or long-term SIPs. See also Risk & allocation and Intraday.

Common mistakes

  • 🎰Treating options like lottery tickets with no plan for premium decay
  • 📊Trading the maximum lot size your margin allows instead of a small, planned size
  • 📉Selling naked options for “easy premium” without stress-testing a bad move
  • 🔄Averaging into a losing futures position with more leverage
  • 📱Copying social-media “tips” without reading contract specs or risk
  • 🏦Skipping emergency cash and core SIPs to fund F&O
