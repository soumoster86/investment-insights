Skills & alternatives · Priority 6

Index funds vs active funds

Two common ways to own a diversified equity (or other) portfolio through mutual funds and ETFs. Understanding the difference helps you read factsheets and expense ratios with clearer eyes. Educational framework only — not a ranking of schemes.

Plain definitions

Index (passive) funds & index ETFs

An index fund or index ETF aims to mirror a published market index (for example a broad large-cap index) by holding the same securities in similar weights. The manager is not trying to “beat” the index after costs; success is measured by how tightly the fund tracks the index (tracking error / tracking difference).

Active funds

An active fund employs a manager or team to select securities, timing, or sector weights with the goal of outperforming a benchmark (or delivering a stated style outcome). Fees are often higher because research and trading costs more. Outperformance is uncertain and not guaranteed in any period.

Side-by-side (simplified)

Aspect Index / passive Active
Goal Match index (minus costs) Beat benchmark or style mandate
Typical costs Often lower expense ratio Often higher expense ratio
Transparency Index rules are public Depends on disclosures & style
Key risk beyond markets Tracking difference, concentration of the index itself Manager risk, style drift, higher fee drag
What “good” looks like Tight tracking, low cost, adequate liquidity (ETFs) Consistent process; past alpha may not repeat

Illustrative. Always read the scheme information document (SID) / KIM.

Why costs matter

Over long SIP horizons, a higher annual expense ratio compounds against you. That does not automatically mean every cheap fund is better — a fund must also fit your risk category — but cost is one of the few things you can compare objectively today.

  • Expense ratio — ongoing fee embedded in NAV.
  • Tracking difference — how much an index product lags (or occasionally leads) its index over time.
  • Exit loads / tax — still apply based on product rules and holding period (see Tax overview).
  • ETF spreads — when buying/selling on exchange, bid–ask and liquidity matter.
Myth check: “Active always beats the market in India” or “index always wins” are both slogans. Evidence varies by category, period, and methodology. Prefer multi-year data and category-appropriate benchmarks over last-year rankings.

How investors often think about the choice

Common educational heuristics (not advice):

  • Core broad-market exposure: many long-term investors use low-cost index funds/ETFs as a simple core.
  • Satellite active sleeves: some add active funds in categories where they believe skill or structural edges may exist — accepting higher cost and uncertainty.
  • Behaviour: a simple plan you stick with often beats a complex plan you abandon.
  • Capacity & style: very large active funds can be constrained; niche active strategies can be volatile.

Asset allocation and emergency buffers still matter more than active-vs-passive debates. Revisit Risk & allocation and Emergency & insurance.

Practical checklist before you pick any fund

  • Category and benchmark match your goal and risk band
  • Expense ratio compared within the same category
  • For index products: tracking difference and AUM / liquidity
  • For active products: process consistency, portfolio overlap with what you already own
  • Direct vs regular plan costs (where relevant)
  • You can stay invested through a 30–40% drawdown in equities without panic-selling

Common mistakes

  • Chasing last year’s top active fund and rotating every year (tax + behaviour drag)
  • Owning five large-cap funds that all hug the same index (hidden index with higher fees)
  • Ignoring that an index itself can be concentrated or expensive in valuation terms
  • Using intraday ETF trading as a substitute for a long-term SIP plan
