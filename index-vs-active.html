Skills & alternatives · Priority 6

Index funds vs active funds

Two common ways to own a diversified portfolio through mutual funds and ETFs. Knowing the difference makes factsheets and expense ratios easier to read. This is a teaching comparison, not a ranking of schemes.

Plain definitions

Index (passive) funds & index ETFs

An index fund or index ETF aims to mirror a published market index (for example a broad large-cap index) by holding the same securities in similar weights. The manager is not trying to “beat” the index after costs. Success is measured by how tightly the fund tracks the index (tracking error or tracking difference).

Active funds

An active fund has a manager or team picking securities, timing, or sector weights with the goal of beating a benchmark (or following a stated style). Fees are often higher because research and trading cost more. Outperformance is uncertain and not guaranteed in any period.

Why costs matter

Over long SIP horizons, a higher annual expense ratio compounds against you. That does not mean every cheap fund is better. The fund still needs to fit your risk category. Cost is one of the few things you can compare objectively today.

  • 💸Expense ratio: ongoing fee embedded in NAV.
  • 📈Tracking difference: how much an index product lags (or occasionally leads) its index over time.
  • 🧾Exit loads and tax: still apply based on product rules and holding period. See the tax overview.
  • 🛟ETF spreads: when you buy or sell on the exchange, bid–ask and liquidity matter.
Myth check: “Active always beats the market in India” and “index always wins” are both slogans. Evidence varies by category, period, and method. Prefer multi-year data and the right benchmark for the category over last-year rankings.

How investors often think about the choice

Common rules of thumb for learning (not advice):

  • 📅Core broad-market exposure: many long-term investors use low-cost index funds or ETFs as a simple base.
  • 🎯Active on the side: some add active funds in categories where they believe skill may help, and accept higher cost and uncertainty.
  • ⏱️Behaviour: a simple plan you stick with often beats a complex plan you abandon.
  • ⚠️Capacity and style: very large active funds can be hard to manage; niche active strategies can be volatile.

Asset allocation and emergency savings still matter more than the active-vs-passive debate. See Risk & allocation and Emergency & insurance.

Practical checklist before you pick any fund

  • 🎯Category and benchmark match your goal and risk level
  • 💸Expense ratio compared within the same category
  • 📉For index products: tracking difference, AUM, and liquidity
  • 🧭For active products: a steady process, and how much it overlaps what you already own
  • 🧾Direct vs regular plan costs (where relevant)
  • 🧘You can stay invested through a 30–40% equity fall without panic-selling

Common mistakes

  • 🏆Chasing last year’s top active fund and switching every year (taxes and bad habits add up)
  • 👯Owning five large-cap funds that all track nearly the same stocks (a pricey “hidden index”)
  • 🎯Forgetting that an index itself can be concentrated or look expensive on valuation
  • ⚡Using intraday ETF trading as a substitute for a long-term SIP plan
