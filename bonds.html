Bonds guide & yield tools

Understand how bonds work, read ratings carefully, estimate current yield and approximate YTM, and sketch a simple bond ladder — educational only, not a recommendation to buy any security.

What is a bond?

A bond is a loan you make to a government or company. In return, the issuer typically pays a coupon (interest) on a schedule and aims to repay the face (par) value at maturity. Bond prices can still move when interest rates or credit quality change — so “fixed income” is not the same as “risk free.”

Why people hold bonds: More predictable cash flows than pure equities, diversification, and a home for money with a known horizon.
Main risks: Credit / default risk, interest-rate risk (prices fall when yields rise), inflation eroding real return, and liquidity risk in some corporate bonds.

Key terms

  • Face / par value — principal used for coupon math (e.g. ₹1,000).
  • Coupon rate — stated interest rate on face value.
  • Market price — what you pay today (premium or discount to par).
  • Current yield — annual coupon ÷ price.
  • YTM — approximate total return if held to maturity (simplified here).
  • Duration / rate risk — longer bonds usually move more when rates change.
  • Callable — issuer may redeem early under terms (complicates yield).
  • Credit rating — agency opinion on default risk, not a guarantee.

Bond ratings & credit risk

Ratings (e.g. AAA, AA, A, BBB and lower) are opinions from agencies such as CRISIL, ICRA, CARE, or India Ratings on an issuer’s ability to meet payments. Higher ratings usually mean lower credit risk and often lower yields; lower ratings mean higher risk of default.

  • Safety trade-off: chasing yield without reading the rating rationale is a common trap.
  • Ratings can change: upgrades/downgrades move prices and liquidity.
  • Not a buy signal: AAA does not remove interest-rate or reinvestment risk.

How to invest in bonds

  1. Complete KYC and open a Demat / trading account if you want exchange-traded bonds or SGBs.
  2. For government securities, explore RBI Retail Direct (official portal).
  3. Decide: single bonds vs debt mutual funds / bond ETFs for diversification.
  4. Match tenure to when you need money; avoid long-duration credit for short goals.
  5. Read rating, call features, tax treatment, and secondary-market liquidity before buying.

Bond laddering strategy

Bond laddering means holding bonds that mature in a sequence (e.g. every year). When a rung matures, you can reinvest into a new long rung — spreading reinvestment risk and creating staggered cash flows.

  1. Pick ladder length and number of rungs.
  2. Split capital (equal rungs is the simplest starting rule).
  3. As each bond matures, reinvest or spend according to your plan.

Maturing cash at the short end can be rolled to the long end or used for a goal.

Portfolio blend: Many investors combine sovereign / high-grade debt with equity SIPs. Short goals lean shorter bonds or FDs; long goals can take more equity — see Risk & allocation.

Sample bond products (educational)

Categories people research — not a buy list. Rates, tax rules, and open issues change; verify with RBI / issuers / exchanges.

RBI Floating Rate Savings Bonds
  • Government-linked savings bond product (rules notified by RBI / GoI)
  • Floating coupon; check the latest rate before applying
  • Typically long lock-in / limited liquidity vs exchange bonds
  • Conservative income sleeve for some households
Tax-free bonds (when available)
  • Interest may be tax-free under specific notified series
  • Often long tenure; new issues are intermittent
  • Secondary market prices can trade above/below par
  • Confirm tax treatment for your situation with a professional
Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGB)
  • Gold-price-linked instrument with a small interest component historically
  • Issued in windows; may trade on exchange after listing
  • Different risks from pure debt bonds
  • Details: Gold investing guide
State Development Loans (SDLs)
  • Debt of state governments
  • Yields set in auctions; vary over time
  • Accessible via G-Sec platforms / some brokers
  • Still study liquidity and your holding period

Common mistakes

  • Assuming “bond = bank FD guarantee” for all corporate credit
  • Buying long-duration bonds with money needed soon
  • Ignoring call features, tax treatment, and secondary-market spreads
  • Chasing high yield without reading the rating report
  • Forgetting that debt fund NAVs can fall when rates rise

Practical tips

  • Prefer higher credit quality for money you cannot afford to lose.
  • Match maturity to goals; use laddering to spread reinvestment risk.
  • Compare after-tax outcomes carefully (tax-free vs taxable).
  • Use debt funds when you want diversification and liquidity of units — with different risks than a single bond held to maturity.
  • Keep emergency money outside long lock-ins — Emergency fund basics.
