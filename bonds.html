Bonds guide & yield tools

How bonds work in India, how to read ratings, and simple tools for current yield, approximate YTM, and a bond ladder. For learning only. Not a recommendation to buy any security.

What is a bond?

A bond is a loan you make to a government or company. The issuer usually pays a coupon (interest) on a schedule and aims to repay the face (par) value at maturity. Bond prices can still rise or fall when interest rates or credit quality change. “Fixed income” is not the same as “risk free.”

Why people hold bonds: Cash flows are often more predictable than stocks. Bonds can diversify a portfolio and suit money you will need on a known date.
Main risks: Default risk, interest-rate risk (prices often fall when yields rise), inflation cutting real returns, and poor liquidity in some corporate bonds.

Key terms

  • 💡Face / par value: Principal used to calculate the coupon (e.g. ₹1,000).
  • ✨Coupon rate: Stated interest rate on face value.
  • 📈Market price: What you pay today, which may be above or below par.
  • ⭐Current yield: Annual coupon divided by price.
  • ✨YTM: Rough total return if you hold to maturity (simplified on this page).
  • ⚠️Duration / rate risk: Longer bonds usually move more when rates change.
  • 🛡️Callable: The issuer may redeem early under the bond’s terms, which affects yield.
  • 📅Credit rating: An agency’s view of default risk, not a guarantee.

Bond ratings & credit risk

Ratings such as AAA, AA, A, and BBB (and lower) are opinions from agencies like CRISIL, ICRA, CARE, or India Ratings on whether an issuer can meet payments. Higher ratings usually mean lower credit risk and often lower yields. Lower ratings mean a higher chance of default.

  • 📋Safety trade-off: Chasing a higher yield without reading why the bond is rated that way is a common trap.
  • 🛟Ratings can change: Upgrades and downgrades move prices and how easy it is to sell.
  • ⚠️Not a buy signal: AAA still carries interest-rate and reinvestment risk.

How to invest in bonds

  1. 🪪Complete KYC and open a Demat / trading account if you want exchange-traded bonds or SGBs.
  2. 🏛️For government securities, use the official RBI Retail Direct portal.
  3. 🔀Choose the form: single bonds, or debt mutual funds / bond ETFs if you want a diversified mix.
  4. 📅Match tenure to when you need the money. Avoid long-duration credit for short goals.
  5. 🔍Before buying, read the rating, call features, tax treatment, and how easy it is to sell later.

Bond laddering strategy

Bond laddering means holding bonds that mature one after another (for example, every year). When a rung matures, you can reinvest into a new longer rung. That spreads reinvestment risk and creates cash coming due at different times.

  1. 🪜Pick the ladder length and how many rungs you want.
  2. ⚖️Split the capital. Equal rungs is the simplest place to start.
  3. 🔄On maturity, reinvest or spend based on your plan.

Cash from the shortest rung can be rolled into a longer one or used for a goal.

In a full portfolio: Many people mix government or high-grade debt with equity SIPs. Short goals often suit shorter bonds or FDs. Longer goals can take more equity. See Risk & allocation.

Sample bond products (educational)

Sample data Last reviewed 2026-07-09 · yields and issues change; verify with issuers.

Product types people research. This is not a buy list. Rates, tax rules, and open issues change. Verify with RBI, issuers, and exchanges.

RBI Floating Rate Savings Bonds
  • Government-linked savings bond (rules set by RBI / GoI)
  • Floating coupon; check the latest rate before applying
  • Usually a long lock-in and less liquid than exchange-traded bonds
  • Some households use them for more conservative income
Tax-free bonds (when available)
  • Interest may be tax-free on specific notified series
  • Often long tenure; new issues are not always open
  • Secondary market prices can trade above or below par
  • Confirm tax treatment for your case with a professional
Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGB)
  • Linked to gold prices, with a small interest amount under past schemes
  • Issued in windows; may trade on the exchange after listing
  • Risks differ from pure debt bonds
  • Details: Gold investing guide
State Development Loans (SDLs)
  • Debt issued by state governments
  • Yields are set in auctions and change over time
  • Available via G-Sec platforms and some brokers
  • Check liquidity and how long you plan to hold

How to read this
  • Extra yield over G-Secs is not free. It pays for default and liquidity risk.
  • Even AAA bonds can fall in price when interest rates rise.
  • Check live yields on the exchange or RBI Retail Direct before you act.

For learning only. Not a recommendation to buy any issuer or rating group.

Common mistakes

  • 🏦Treating every corporate bond like a bank FD guarantee
  • ⏳Buying long-duration bonds with money you need soon
  • 📎Skipping call features, tax treatment, and resale spreads
  • 🎣Chasing a high yield without reading the rating report
  • 📉Forgetting that debt fund NAVs can fall when rates rise

Practical tips

  • 🛡️Prefer higher credit quality for money you cannot afford to lose.
  • 🪜Match maturity to your goals. Laddering can spread reinvestment risk.
  • 🧾Compare after-tax results carefully (tax-free vs taxable).
  • 📦Debt funds can give diversification and unit liquidity, but they do not behave like one bond held to maturity.
  • 🛟Keep emergency money out of long lock-ins. See Emergency fund basics.
